## FORMAT 1 — AUDIO OVERVIEW: DEEP DIVE (Two Hosts)

**NotebookLM Settings:**

- Format: Deep Dive
- Length: Default
- Language: English

---

### DEEP DIVE PROMPT A — The Career Arc

*Best for: Candidates with strong career progression and measurable results*

Two hiring managers are having a genuine conversation about this candidate after reviewing their file. They are not reading the resume -- they are discussing what they actually found. Start with the result that stops you cold. The number that makes you look up from the page. Build the story chronologically from where this person started to where they are now. Focus on what they built, what they fixed, and what they left behind that still runs without them. Talk about the range -- can they lead from the floor and the boardroom? End with the question both hosts are asking themselves: why is this person still available and how fast should we move?

---

### DEEP DIVE PROMPT B — The Problem Solver

*Best for: Candidates who specialize in fixing broken operations or turning around underperforming teams*

Two experienced hiring managers are debating this candidate's file. One opens with the most impressive turnaround or fix in the resume -- the moment where things were broken and this person made them work. The other pushes deeper -- how did they actually do it? What did it take? Who did they have to bring along? Unpack the methodology not just the result. Connect the dots between every role -- is there a pattern here? A signature move? A way this person consistently operates that makes them different from the hundred other resumes on the desk? Close with what kind of company needs exactly this person right now.

---

### DEEP DIVE PROMPT C — The Leadership Story

*Best for: Senior candidates applying for director or VP level roles*

Two senior executives are reviewing this candidate for a leadership role. One host focuses on the results -- the KPIs, the numbers, the scale. The other focuses on the human story -- how did they build their teams? How do they handle underperformers? What kind of culture do they create? Weave both threads together. A great operator who cannot lead people is a technician. A great leader who cannot deliver results is a motivational speaker. This person needs to be both. Make the case that they are -- or push back if the evidence is not there. End with a clear verdict: leadership material or individual contributor?

---

### DEEP DIVE PROMPT D — The Builder

*Best for: Candidates who have built operations from scratch multiple times*

Two hiring managers are excited about something in this file -- the number of times this person has built something from nothing. Start there. Count them. Name them. Discuss what it actually takes to launch an operation from lease signing to first product in market. Talk about the skills that only come from doing that -- the tolerance for ambiguity, the ability to make decisions without a playbook, the discipline to build systems that scale. Compare that to candidates who have only maintained what others built. End with the specific type of company that needs exactly this person -- and what they would do there in the first 90 days.

---

### DEEP DIVE PROMPT E — The Skeptic and The Champion

*Best for: Candidates with non-linear career paths or gaps that need contextualizing*

One host is a champion for this candidate. The other is a skeptic. The champion goes first -- makes the strongest possible case using only the evidence in the file. The skeptic pushes back with the tough questions a real hiring committee would ask. Why the gap? Why did they leave that role? Can they handle a bigger scope? The champion defends with specific examples and evidence. Keep it sharp and professional -- this is not an attack, it is due diligence. By the end both hosts should land somewhere honest -- this candidate is worth a conversation, or they are not, and here is exactly why.

## FORMAT 2 — AUDIO OVERVIEW: BRIEF

**NotebookLM Settings:**

- Format: Brief
- Length: Short
- Language: English

---

### BRIEF PROMPT A — The Hook

*Best for: LinkedIn profiles, email signatures, quick first impressions*

Create a tight, compelling audio brief that a busy hiring manager would listen to between meetings. Open with the single most impressive result in this candidate's career -- the number or achievement that makes you stop scrolling. Give it context in one sentence. Then land on two more proof points that show this is not a fluke -- it is a pattern. Close with one sharp reason this person deserves a conversation. Under 90 seconds. No fluff. No corporate speak. Every word earns its place.

---

### BRIEF PROMPT B — The Commute Brief

*Best for: Hiring managers who consume candidates on the go*

This is a candidate briefing for a hiring manager on their morning commute. They have 2 minutes before they arrive. Give them exactly what they need to know. Who is this person in one sentence. What have they consistently delivered across their career in two sentences. What makes them different from the other candidates on the desk in one sentence. What is the one thing a hiring manager would regret not knowing about them. Tight, clear, and confident. Sound like a trusted colleague giving a recommendation -- not a resume being read aloud.

---

### BRIEF PROMPT C — The Elevator Pitch

*Best for: Candidates who want a punchy asset for job applications*

Two hosts have 90 seconds to make the case for this candidate to a hiring committee that has already seen 50 resumes today. They need to cut through immediately. Open with the career in one line -- not a job title, but what this person actually does better than almost anyone. Hit the top two numbers that prove it. Name the industries and environments where they have delivered. Close with the single strongest reason to move this candidate to the next round. Fast, sharp, and memorable.

---

### BRIEF PROMPT D — The Specialist Brief

*Best for: Candidates with deep expertise in a specific niche*

Create a brief audio profile that establishes this candidate as a specialist -- not a generalist. What is the specific thing they do better than almost anyone in their field? Where did that expertise come from? What results has it produced? What kind of problem walks through the door and this is exactly the person you want in the room? Under 2 minutes. The goal is for a hiring manager to finish this audio and immediately think: I know exactly where this person fits and I need to talk to them.

---

### BRIEF PROMPT E — The Culture Add

*Best for: Candidates whose leadership philosophy and work style are strong differentiators*

Skip the resume recap. A hiring manager can read. What they cannot read is who this person actually is as a leader and colleague. In 90 seconds tell the story of how this candidate leads -- how they build teams, how they handle pressure, what they need from a manager, and what their people say about them. Ground it in one specific example from their career. Close with the kind of company culture where this person thrives and why that matters for the role they are targeting.

## FORMAT 3 — INFOGRAPHIC

**NotebookLM Settings:**

- Orientation: Landscape
- Visual Style: Professional
- Level of Detail: Standard
- Language: English

---

### INFOGRAPHIC PROMPT A — The Career Timeline

*Best for: Candidates with strong linear career progression*

Create a professional career timeline infographic for this candidate. The visual spine is their career chronology -- company names, titles, and dates on a clean horizontal or vertical timeline. At each major role highlight the single most impressive result with a stat or achievement callout. Use a dark professional color scheme -- navy and gold or charcoal and blue. Hero stats should be large and bold. Career arc should read as clear upward progression. Include a brief career summary at the top. No cartoons. No clip art. Executive and data-driven in tone.

---

### INFOGRAPHIC PROMPT B — The Impact Numbers

*Best for: Candidates with strong quantitative results across multiple roles*

Create a data-forward career infographic that leads with numbers. The hero section should feature the 3-4 most impressive career statistics -- large, bold, impossible to miss. Below that show the career arc with company names and roles. Include a callout section for awards, certifications, or notable achievements. Use a professional color palette -- navy, charcoal, and gold. Typography should be clean and minimal. The visual hierarchy should guide the eye from the biggest number to the career context to the supporting details. Think executive profile meets data dashboard.

---

### INFOGRAPHIC PROMPT C — The Two Era Career

*Best for: Candidates with a clear early career foundation and later strategic evolution*

Create a split-panel career infographic showing two distinct eras of this candidate's career. Left panel: the foundational years -- where they built their craft, the early roles, the technical expertise developed. Right panel: the strategic era -- where they led at scale, the scope expanded, the impact compounded. Each panel should have its own headline that captures the era in 3-4 words. Key stats from each era highlighted. Center spine shows the transition moment. Professional color scheme. Clean typography. The story of someone who earned the right to lead.

---

### INFOGRAPHIC PROMPT D — The Specialist Profile

*Best for: Candidates with deep domain expertise in a specific field*

Create a domain expertise infographic that positions this candidate as a specialist. Top section: their area of expertise stated clearly in bold. Middle section: three columns showing depth (years of experience), breadth (industries and environments), and proof (key results). Bottom section: a skills and certifications bar with their core competencies. Color scheme should feel authoritative and credible -- dark navy or deep charcoal with white typography. This is not a resume. It is a credential document that establishes why this person is the expert in the room.

---

### INFOGRAPHIC PROMPT E — The Leadership Profile

*Best for: Senior candidates targeting director or VP level roles*

Create a leadership profile infographic for a senior candidate. Lead with their leadership philosophy in a pull quote at the top -- one sentence that captures how they lead. Below that: a three-column section showing People (team sizes led, hiring and development record), Performance (KPI achievements and operational results), and Process (systems built, improvements made). Bottom section: notable achievements, awards, and career highlights. Professional and executive in tone. Dark color palette. No cartoons or decorative elements. This should feel like something a board of directors would review.

### Prompt Engineering Notes:

- Always specify "no corporate speak" and "no hype" -- NotebookLM tends toward generic if not constrained
- Always specify a target length -- it runs long without instruction
- Always give the hosts a specific narrative angle -- facts to recite produce robotic output
- Always specify visual style explicitly for infographics -- default styles are too casual
- The best prompts give the AI a story to tell, not a resume to summarize
